The Problem

WLK (Weight Loss by Kiyomi) is a herbal detox brand selling NAFDAC registered cleanse products made from natural ingredients like avocado seed, caralluma fimbriata and garcinia cambogia. When the client came to me, the entire business was running through Instagram captions and WhatsApp DMs. There was no catalog, no fixed pricing page, no way for a new customer to see the full product range at a glance, and no consistent way to actually complete an order.

That created a real cost. Interested customers would ask a question in the comments, get lost in the DM queue, and never convert. The client needed a single, credible destination where someone could land, understand the product line in under a minute, and check out without waiting on a reply.

My Role

I ran this project solo, end to end. There was no separate designer, developer or project manager, all three hats were mine, which meant every decision, from what the store sold first to how the code was structured, had to be made and owned by one person.

Research

Before touching any layout, I went through the client's existing sales conversations to understand what customers actually asked about most. That research shaped almost every early decision on the site.

  • Repeated questions — Customers consistently asked whether the product was "safe," whether it was registered, and what the actual difference was between the bottle sizes. That told me trust signals needed to sit above the fold, not in a footer.
  • Buying pattern — Most orders bundled a bottle with the detox capsule add-on, which meant the store needed combo kits as first-class products, not an afterthought upsell.
  • Local context — Almost all customers were ordering from Lagos and paying informally through bank transfer or on delivery, which meant a heavy, generic payment gateway integration wasn't the highest priority for version one, a fast, familiar checkout was.
  • Competitor scan — Comparable wellness storefronts either looked overly clinical (losing the natural, herbal feel) or too informal (losing credibility). The gap was a store that felt premium and natural at the same time.

Design

With the research in hand, I moved into structuring the actual product experience before writing any interface code.

Wireframes

I sketched the page as a single scrolling flow rather than multiple pages, since most wellness shoppers browse on mobile and don't want to navigate between separate product pages for a small catalog.

[ Hero: product shot + tagline + CTA ]
[ Trust badge row: Natural · Burns Fat · Lagos Delivery · NAFDAC · No Additives ]
[ Section: Singles — Lite / Core cleanse cards ]
[ Section: Detox Add-On — capsule upsell card ]
[ Section: Combo Kits — bundled bottles ]
[ Section: Flagship — 7-Day Reset Program, largest card ]
[ Sticky cart icon + drawer ]
[ Footer: WhatsApp contact + NAFDAC reg. number ]
Mockups

High fidelity screens followed the wireframe almost exactly, with product photography treated as the hero element on every card and pricing always visible without a click. Card hierarchy was deliberately tiered, singles first, add-on second, combos third, flagship program last as the biggest visual moment on the page.

Design System

A small, deliberate system: deep greens for a natural, herbal feel, generous spacing so the page never feels like a cluttered catalog, and one consistent card component reused across every product tier.

Development

Frontend

Built with plain HTML, CSS and JavaScript for speed and simplicity. Product cards render from a single JS data array, so adding or editing a product means editing one object, not the markup.

Backend

No dedicated server for version one. Cart state and order handoff are handled client side, keeping hosting simple and the store fast to load on mobile data.

Database

Product data (name, price, size, description) lives in a structured JS object rather than a database, deliberately, since the catalog is small and changes infrequently. This kept load times near instant.

Authentication

No customer accounts by design. Adding login would have added friction to a store where speed to order matters more than saved profiles, so checkout stays guest-only.

Cart & Checkout Logic

I built a persistent cart with add, remove and quantity logic, a running total, and a checkout step that formats the full order (products, quantities, total) into a pre-filled WhatsApp message.

Deployment

Deployed directly to the kiyomistore.com domain with static hosting, no build pipeline required, which means updates ship in minutes.

Challenges

Payment gateway vs. speed to launch

The obvious "correct" build would include a full payment gateway. I made the call to route checkout through WhatsApp instead for version one, since that matched how the brand's customers already bought and got the store live in days instead of weeks. It's a decision I'd revisit once order volume justifies the added complexity.

Selling a bundle without confusing the customer

With six product variants across singles, an add-on, combos and a flagship kit, the risk was decision paralysis. I solved this by grouping products into clearly labeled tiers with a visual "Best Value" cue on the combo and flagship sections, rather than presenting all six as equal, flat options.

Trust without a sales team

An ingestible wellness product needs credibility a normal product doesn't. I addressed this by surfacing the NAFDAC registration number and safety notes (not suitable for pregnant women or children under 12) directly in the footer and near the hero, rather than treating that as legal fine print.

Mobile Optimized

The store replaced a fragmented DM-based sales process with a single, shoppable destination. Customers can now browse the full catalog, understand pricing instantly, and reach checkout without waiting on a reply, which directly shortened the path from "interested" to "ordered." Because the site is a lightweight static build, it loads fast even on average mobile connections in Lagos, which matters when most traffic comes from Instagram link clicks on mobile data.

Lessons Learned

The biggest lesson from this build was that the "complete" version of a product isn't always the right version to ship first. A full payment gateway and customer account system would have taken this store weeks longer to launch, for a customer base that was already comfortable ordering through WhatsApp. Shipping the simpler, faster version got the brand an actual working store immediately, and gave me a clear, validated reason to build out payments later instead of guessing upfront what the business actually needed.
